## Aphids

### Plants at risk in this yard

- **Cherry bush** — black cherry aphids (*Myzus cerasi*); historically peaks late May–June
- **Kale and brassicas (PS2, FS2)** — green peach aphid and others; watch undersides of leaves
- **Hops (front left)** — aphids common on new growth in early season
- **Peach tree** — monitor at bud break; less severe than cherry but same species range

### About black cherry aphids

Small, very dark (nearly black) with some winged adults. Colonies form on the newest shoot tips in late spring. Leaves curl inward around the colony, protecting it from sprays — which is why catching them early matters. Excrete honeydew → sooty mold on leaves below. Population usually crashes mid-to-late summer as natural predators (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps) catch up.

### Control protocol

**Step 1 — prune the worst tips (do first).**
Any shoot where leaves have fully curled around the colony: cut it off and bag it. Don't compost — that just moves the colony. This removes the most protected populations that sprays cannot reach.

**Step 2 — water blast.**
Strong hose stream on all affected shoots, focusing on shoot tips and leaf undersides. Early morning so leaves dry before peak sun. Aphids do not re-climb easily once knocked off.

**Step 3 — insecticidal soap or neem oil (5–7 day intervals, 2–3 rounds).**
Contact-kill only — coverage matters more than concentration. Coat shoot tips and leaf undersides thoroughly. Apply in the evening or early morning to avoid leaf burn. Both are safe on fruiting trees with no meaningful pre-harvest interval.

**Step 4 — ant control.**
If ants are observed climbing the trunk, they are farming the aphid colony and actively protecting it from predators. Wrap trunk with sticky barrier (Tanglefoot or similar) to cut off access.

**What to avoid:**
- Neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, acetamiprid, dinotefuran) — systemic, will get into fruit, and kill beneficial insects.
- Broad-spectrum insecticides generally — ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps are your best long-term control; preserve them.

## Grasshoppers

### About (Front Range context)

A perennial problem on the Denver Front Range, especially in drought years. Grasshoppers migrate in from surrounding lawn, fields, and roadsides — isolated raised beds are attractive targets. Damage peaks July–September when adult populations are at their highest and garden plants are at full size. A single wave can defoliate a raised bed in a day.

No single method is sufficient. The most effective approach layers early biological control with physical barriers during peak pressure.

### Control protocol

#### Early season — June (nymphs, small and slow)

**Nosema locustae bait (Semaspore / Nolo Bait) — highest-leverage action.**
Biological control; the spore infects grasshoppers and spreads within the population as infected individuals are eaten. Most effective on young nymphs (apply when nymphs are first observed, typically late May–mid June in Denver). Apply to lawn and surrounding grass areas, not just the garden beds — grasshoppers pick it up while feeding on grass. One application per season; takes 3–6 weeks to reduce population. Buy in spring; availability varies. Apply before the population becomes predominantly adults.

**Hand-picking.**
When pressure is still low in early June, daily removal by hand before populations become mobile adults makes a meaningful dent.

#### Mid-season — physical barriers

**Row covers.**
The raised beds already have row cover infrastructure (FS2, PS2, PS1). Deploy covers as a physical barrier when grasshopper pressure starts — they can't chew through row cover fabric. Use fine mesh rather than frost cloth alone if grasshoppers are particularly aggressive. Timing: covers go on proactively at first sighting, not after damage starts.

**Kaolin clay (Surround WP).**
Coat leaf surfaces; grasshoppers avoid feeding on treated foliage. Organic and safe on fruiting crops. Needs reapplication after rain. Most useful on uncovered crops (FS1, front yard, trees).

#### Active infestation — contact treatments

**Spinosad** (organic, derived from soil bacteria).
Most effective on small nymphs; less so on large adults. Apply early morning when grasshoppers are sluggish. Short pre-harvest interval; safe on fruiting crops. Spinosad is also a good choice for caterpillars (cabbage worms on Brussels sprouts).

**Pyrethrin spray** (organic, short residual).
Fast knockdown on contact; no systemic activity. Apply when grasshoppers are concentrated on plants. Residual is hours, not days — follow up needed.

#### Last resort — conventional

**Carbaryl (Sevin).**
Highly effective. Broad-spectrum: kills beneficial insects, pollinators, natural enemies. Do not apply to flowering plants or near the greenhouse. Use only on non-flowering crops (e.g., kale, onions) after organic options have failed. Avoid entirely on fruiting trees and the front yard (pollinator habitat).

## Other pests to watch

### Peach leaf curl (*Taphrina deformans*)

Fungal — not an insect, but monitored alongside pests. Causes red/puckered blistering on new peach leaves in early spring. No cure once symptoms appear in a given season; the treatment window is **dormant season only** (fall after leaf drop through late winter before bud swell). If observed in 2026, plan a dormant copper fungicide spray in fall 2026 or late winter 2027 before bud break.

- 2026 status: No symptoms observed as of 2026-06-01 (see peach pruning log in [`CARE.md`](CARE.md#peach-pruning-log)).

### Cabbage worms / imported cabbageworm (*Pieris rapae*)

Affects Brussels sprouts (FS2) and kale (PS2). Small green caterpillars; look for irregular holes in leaves and frass. Control: hand-pick; **Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) spray** is the organic standard — apply to leaf surfaces, particularly undersides. Reapply after rain. Safe on all crops; does not affect beneficials or pollinators.

### Squash vine borer

Watch for on spaghetti squash (front right) from late June onward. Adult moth lays eggs at stem base; larvae bore into the stem. Signs: sudden wilting of a vine that was healthy. Hard to treat once inside the stem. Prevention: row cover through flowering (then remove for pollination), or wrap stem base with foil as a physical barrier.
